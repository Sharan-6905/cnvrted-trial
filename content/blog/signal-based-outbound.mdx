Most outbound starts with a list and asks *who do I email.*

Signal-based outbound starts with a trigger and asks *who just told me they're ready.* This is the whole difference, and it changes everything downstream — who you contact, when, and what you say.

Here's how to actually run it.

## What a signal is

A signal is any public evidence that a buyer's situation just changed in a way that makes your product relevant. Not "they fit the ICP." That's static. A signal is *temporal* — it has a shelf life, and it points at a window.

Good signals share three traits:

- **They're recent.** A signal from six weeks ago is a lead. A signal from yesterday is an opening.
- **They're specific to your problem.** "Raised a Series A" is generic. "Hiring three SDRs after raising a Series A" tells you they're about to feel the exact pain you fix.
- **They imply a next action.** The buyer is already moving. You're not creating urgency — you're catching it.

## Where they live

You don't need a database for these. They're in the open.

- **LinkedIn** — job changes into a buying role, hiring for roles that create your need, posts and comments about the problem you solve, engagement on competitor content.
- **Reddit** — the most honest place on the internet. People describe their exact stack, their frustration with the incumbent, and what they wish existed, in their own words, unprompted. B2B buyers vent here before they ever talk to a vendor.
- **X** — real-time complaints, "what do you all use for ___" threads, founders narrating problems as they hit them.

The skill isn't finding one signal. It's watching all three continuously so you catch the moment, not the aftermath.

## How to build a play off a signal

One signal, one play. Don't blend ten signals into one generic sequence — that's just the old list with extra steps.

1. **Pick a trigger.** E.g. *a company posts a job req for their first RevOps hire.*
2. **Write the hypothesis.** What does this signal tell you about their pain? (First RevOps hire → their GTM data is a mess and someone finally owns it.)
3. **Write to the hypothesis, not the company.** Your first line references the trigger and the pain it implies. Not "congrats on the role" — *"first RevOps hire usually means the pipeline data has outgrown the spreadsheet. That's the exact week most teams start ___."*
4. **Make the ask small and matched to the moment.** Early trigger = light touch. Mid-trigger = direct.

The message reads like you were paying attention, because you were.

## Timing

A signal decays. The value of reaching someone is highest in the first few days after the trigger and drops fast. So the system has to be *fast* — watching in real time, surfacing the signal while the window's open, not in a weekly export.

This is the part manual prospecting can't win. A human can write a great signal-based email. A human cannot watch LinkedIn, Reddit and X continuously across every account and fire within hours. That gap is exactly why this used to be a boutique tactic and not a repeatable motion.

## A simple sequence off one signal

- **Touch 1 (day 0–1):** reference the trigger + the pain it implies. One clear line about why now. No pitch.
- **Touch 2 (day 3):** a proof point tied to that specific pain. Short.
- **Touch 3 (day 6):** a different angle — a resource, a teardown, a peer example. Still no "just following up."
- **Stop.** If the signal was real and the timing was right, three good touches beat twelve generic ones.

## Why this beats volume

Volume assumes the answer is more people. Signal-based outbound assumes the answer is *better timing on fewer people.* Smaller list, higher intent, sharper message, tighter window. Every part of the funnel improves because you started from "they're ready" instead of "they exist."

The old playbook was: cast wide, hope for timing.

The new one is: catch timing, then reach out.
